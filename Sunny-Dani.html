About

Flavius Petrus Sabbatius, later Justinian I, rose from humble origins to become one of the most influential rulers of the Byzantine Empire. Adopted by his uncle Justin I, he assumed the throne in 527 CE.

His reign was marked by sweeping legal reform, ambitious military campaigns, and monumental construction projects including the Hagia Sophia.

Legacy

Justinian I is remembered as one of the most important Byzantine emperors, with a legacy that includes:

  • Legal Legacy: Basis of civil law in Europe
  • Architecture: The Hagia Sophia
  • Military: Reconquered Western territories
  • Religion: Strengthened Christianity

Justinian's Code

The Corpus Juris Civilis organized Roman law into four parts:

  • Codex: Imperial statutes
  • Digest: Juristic writings
  • Institutes: Law textbook
  • Novellae: New laws

This framework standardized laws on crimes, property, contracts, and marriage. Its influence shaped European law for centuries.

Legal Theories

Justinian aligned with natural law theory, viewing law as reflecting divine and natural justice, not just imperial command. His legal philosophy emphasized:

  • The emperor as the ultimate source of law
  • Strict obedience to imperial authority
  • Legal uniformity across the empire
  • Integration of Christian principles into Roman law

With no separation between church and state, laws were justified by divine order, reinforcing imperial authority with moral legitimacy.

Civil Disobedience

In Justinian's empire, disobedience was treated as rebellion against both state and divine authority. The Nika Riots demonstrated that even peaceful protest was met with violent suppression.

With law seen as flowing from God through the emperor, there was no room for civil disobedience as a moral act. Society valued order and divine authority over individual conscience.

Excerpt

"For as marriage is so advantageous that it seems to provide an artificial immortality for the human race, in that, by the procreation of children, families are constantly renewed, and if God, in his mercy, by this means, confers an eternal existence upon our species, as far as is possible, it is only proper for Us to devote the greatest care to matters relating to marriage."

This excerpt shows Justinian's alignment with natural law theory, grounding marriage law in divine purpose rather than mere imperial decree. It highlights how Christianity shaped Byzantine law and expected conformity.
